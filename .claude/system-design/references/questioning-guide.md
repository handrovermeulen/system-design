# Questioning Guide

This guide structures the conversation when an operator runs `/system:new-system`.
The goal is to surface the structure of their system through plain-language questions.
The Meadows framework (stocks, flows, feedback loops) drives the sequence. The operator never sees those terms.

## Phase 1: Open

Ask: "What system do you want to design?"

Wait. Let them dump their mental model. Do not interrupt. Do not redirect.
Their first answer reveals what they think the system is. That framing is data.

Follow up on whatever they said. Ask about what excited them, what problem sparked this, what they mean by vague terms.

## Phase 2: Purpose and Boundaries

- "What is this system's job? What does it produce?"
- "Who are the actors? Who triggers things, who receives outputs, who operates it?"
- "Where does the system start and end? What is inside your control? What is outside?"
- "Does this system exist today in some form? What works? What is broken?"

Push for specifics. "Marketing pipeline" is not a system. "The process that turns a stranger into a booked call" is closer.

## Phase 3: What Builds Up

- "What builds up in this system over time? Think: leads, content pieces, revenue, trust, backlog, knowledge, inventory, attention."
- "For each of those: what is the current level? Where do you want it to be?"
- "Which one matters most? If one of these is healthy, which one makes everything else work?"

Probe for things they did not mention. If they said "leads" but not "trust" or "reputation," ask whether those matter.

## Phase 4: What Moves

- "What fills each of those? What drains them?"
- "What triggers each movement? Is it manual, scheduled, event-driven, or threshold-based?"
- "Where do things connect? Does the output of one process feed into another?"
- "What is the typical volume? Per day, per week, per month?"

This phase maps the flows. Every accumulation has inflows and outflows. If they cannot name an outflow, that is a finding: something builds up with no release.

## Phase 5: Self-Correction

- "How would you know if [thing that builds up] is too high or too low?"
- "What do you do when it drifts? Who acts? How fast?"
- "Is anything self-reinforcing? More of X leads to more of Y leads to more of X?"
- "How long between noticing a problem and fixing it?"

This phase surfaces feedback mechanisms. Most systems have few or none. That is the gap this skill chain exists to close.

## Phase 6: Failure

- "What breaks first when you are overloaded?"
- "Where do things get stuck?"
- "What happens when a key person is unavailable?"
- "Have you ever had a situation where the system produced exactly what was measured, but not what was actually wanted?"

The last question detects the "measuring the wrong thing" trap. Operators often design systems that optimise for a metric that does not align with the real goal.

## Phase 7: Leverage

- "If you could change one thing about how this system works, what would have the biggest impact?"
- "What rules govern this system? Who set them? Can they change?"
- "What information is missing? What would change if everyone involved could see [X]?"

This phase surfaces leverage points. The operator's intuition about "one thing to change" often points directly at the highest-leverage intervention.

## When to Probe Deeper

- Vague language: "we handle that" or "it just works" or "someone takes care of it." Push: "Who specifically? What do they do? What triggers them?"
- Missing actors: if nobody is named as responsible, the system has a gap.
- Missing failure paths: if the operator cannot describe what happens when something breaks, the system has no resilience.
- Circular references: "A feeds B, B feeds A." Ask whether that is intentional (reinforcing cycle) or accidental (dependency loop).

## When to Move On

Move to the next phase when:
- The operator has given specific, concrete answers (not generalities)
- You can name the key elements in that phase (the actors, the accumulations, the flows)
- Probing further yields repetition rather than new information

## Decision Gate

When you have enough clarity to write SYSTEM-MAP.md, OUTCOMES.md, and DESIGN.md, offer to proceed.

Signal: you can describe the system back to the operator in 3-4 sentences and they say "yes, that is it."

Do not rush this. The questioning phase is the most leveraged moment in the entire chain. Deep questioning here means better design, better verification, better builds.
