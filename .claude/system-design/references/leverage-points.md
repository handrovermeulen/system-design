# Leverage Points

Where to intervene in a system for maximum impact. Ordered from weakest (12) to strongest (1). Based on Donella Meadows' hierarchy, translated to business system language.

The key insight: most people intervene at the weakest points (tweaking numbers) because they are the easiest to change. The strongest interventions (changing goals and mindset) are harder but produce fundamentally different outcomes.

## Tier 1: Tweaking Numbers and Sizes (Weakest)

### 12. Adjusting constants

Changing numbers like prices, targets, budgets, headcount. The easiest lever and the least effective. The system structure stays the same.

**Example:** Increasing ad spend by 20%. The pipeline structure does not change. You get proportionally more of whatever the system already produces.

**When to use:** When the system works well and just needs calibration.

### 11. Changing buffer sizes

Making accumulations larger or smaller to absorb variation. Bigger buffers add stability but reduce responsiveness.

**Example:** Increasing the content backlog from 1 week to 4 weeks. Absorbs disruptions but delays feedback on what is working.

**When to use:** When the system oscillates because accumulations are too small to handle normal variation.

### 10. Changing physical structure

Rearranging the components of the system. Expensive and slow to change once built.

**Example:** Splitting a monolithic process into parallel subsystems. Restructuring the team. Changing the toolstack.

**When to use:** When the current structure physically cannot support the desired behavior.

## Tier 2: Changing Timing and Correction Strength (Moderate)

### 9. Adjusting delays

Changing how long it takes for information or material to move through the system. Delays are often the hidden cause of oscillation and overreaction.

**Example:** Moving from monthly reporting to daily dashboards. The correction cycle shrinks from 30 days to 1 day.

**When to use:** When the system reacts too slowly to problems, or overcorrects because feedback arrives late.

### 8. Strengthening balancing corrections

Making self-correction mechanisms stronger relative to the problems they address.

**Example:** Adding automated alerts when lead response time exceeds 4 hours, instead of relying on weekly manual review.

**When to use:** When the system drifts because corrections are too weak or too slow relative to the forces pushing it off target.

### 7. Weakening amplifiers

Reducing the strength of self-reinforcing cycles that drive exponential growth or collapse.

**Example:** Capping the bonus structure so top performers do not absorb all resources. Limiting how much budget automatically shifts to the best-performing channel.

**When to use:** When a reinforcing cycle is creating winner-takes-all dynamics or unsustainable growth.

## Tier 3: Changing Information, Rules, and Structure (Strong)

### 6. Changing information flows

Changing who has access to what information. Often the most cost-effective intervention. Adding a feedback loop where none existed.

**Example:** Giving the sales team visibility into delivery capacity. Giving the founder a real-time dashboard instead of monthly reports. Surfacing customer churn data to the product team.

**When to use:** When decisions are being made without the data needed to make them well. When information exists but does not reach the people who need it.

### 5. Changing rules

Changing the incentives, constraints, and permissions that govern behavior. Rules determine who can do what, under what conditions, with what consequences.

**Example:** Changing approval authority so the team can publish content without founder sign-off. Adding a rule that no client engagement starts without a completed tech access doc.

**When to use:** When the system produces the wrong behavior because the rules reward the wrong things.

### 4. Allowing self-organization

Giving the system the power to change its own structure. Adding, removing, or reorganizing components without external intervention.

**Example:** Letting the AI agent create new skills when it detects a recurring pattern. Allowing teams to restructure their own workflows.

**When to use:** When the system needs to adapt to conditions you cannot predict in advance.

## Tier 4: Changing Goals, Mindset, and Paradigm (Strongest)

### 3. Changing the goal

Changing what the system is trying to achieve. Everything below this point (rules, information, structure, corrections, numbers) serves the goal. Change the goal and everything else must realign.

**Example:** Shifting from "maximise revenue" to "maximise client retention." The same system produces fundamentally different behavior.

**When to use:** When the system works perfectly but produces the wrong outcome.

### 2. Changing the mindset

Changing the beliefs and assumptions from which the system's goals arise. The mindset determines what goals seem reasonable.

**Example:** Shifting from "we compete on price" to "we compete on infrastructure." From "AI replaces people" to "AI amplifies people." The goals, rules, and structure all follow from the mindset.

**When to use:** When the goals themselves are wrong because they arise from a flawed mental model.

### 1. Transcending paradigms

The ability to operate without being locked into any single paradigm. Holding all mental models as tools, not truths.

**Example:** Recognising that "grow fast" and "grow sustainably" are both paradigms with tradeoffs, not universal truths. Choosing deliberately based on context rather than defaulting to one.

**When to use:** When you need to see the system from outside any single framework.
